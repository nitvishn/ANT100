Lecture 2 — 20 January 2022

Popular misconceptions of culture

  • Misconception 1: We have “culture,” while Others don’t.
    • For example, the early portrayals of Indigenous Peoples as lacking Culture and close to Nature.
    • Here, culture is seen as the intellectual side of (Western) civilisation.
    • Defined by ‘classical’ European aesthetic forms (opera, ballet, theatre, literature, art); fixed definition
      • The idea that only certain nations, classes, races, ethnic groups, or educational strata have real “culture” has justified.
      • elitism, racism, and ethnocentrism
    • Also called “High Culture”
    • A specialized and superior form of intellectual and aesthetic practice.
    • Serves to label certain products (e.g., literary or musical works) as the "greatest" or "highest" achievements of humanity.
  • Misconception 2: The Others have culture, while We don’t. Instead, we have technology.
    • For example, think about phrases like “They have so much” or “so rich culture.”
    • Cultural exotism
  • Popular misconceptions: two sides of the same coin
    • All human societies have culture, not more or less.
    • Every single human action, institution, belief, practice and artifact is cultural.
    • Human beings are bound with culture.
  • The origin of the concept of culture
    • “Culture" emerged as a noun that referred first to agriculture (cultured milk) and then was applied to the "cultivation" of human capacities.
    • Following that application, "culture” started to be used for “cultivated” activities to distinguish them from the “uncultured” or “uncivilized” ones.
    • More recently (last 100 years), "culture" also has come to mean the life context of all societies.
  • Culture as a “whole way of life”
    • “ a particular way of life which expresses certain meanings and values not only in art and learning, but also in institutions and ordinary behaviour. The analysis of culture, from such a definition, is the clarification of the meanings and values implicit and explicit in a particular way of life, a particular culture.” (Raymond Williams, 1965)

Anthropological definition of culture

  • “The system of meanings about the nature of experience that are shared by a people and passed from one generation to another” — Robbins, Dowty, Cummings and McCarty, Sociocultural Anthropology
  • “share the same meaning-universe”
  • Human beings as cultural animals
    • Human beings live in cultural worlds that they themselves construct by giving meanings to all facets of their lives.
    • Different groups of people construct different cultural worlds.
    • Differences in cultural beliefs and practices can arise because people participate in different realities
    • Example: What you would eat and what you would not eat are culturally constructed.

Attributes of culture

  • Culture is learned
    • Not genetic, not inherited, not innate
    • Learned by being told, observation and imitation
    • Learned through the process of enculturation (”the process by which a person internalises the behaviour patterns of the culture they live in”).
    • Explicit culture
      • Explicit culture is the cultural knowledge you can talk about.
      • You learn it mostly by hearing it from others, such as from your parents, teachers, peers, etc.
    • Tacit culture
      • Tacit culture is cultural knowledge that people lack words for.
      • You learn it mostly by practice, imitation, and observation. For example, sounds you can distinguish and utter.
  • Culture is symbolic
    • In human behaviour, everything is either biological or cultural.
    • So, if a human behaviour is not biological, it is cultural.
    • Cultural behaviour is learned, but our cultural learning, however, depends on symbols.
    • Our social and cultural world is a series of sign systems similar to languages.
    • We communicate meanings through symbols.
    • A symbol is something, such as an object, gesture, picture, or word that stands for something else.
    • Symbols are signs that have no necessary or natural connection to the things they signify or for which they stand.
    • Humans have the ability to establish arbitrary relations between signs and what they refer to.
    • Language is a symbolic system.
    • There is no necessary or natural connection between the words we say and their meanings.
  • Culture is shared yet contested
    • Members of a cultural group share common beliefs, values, behavioural patterns.
    • They can “decode”, understand, and interpret tacit and explicit signs that circulate in their cultural group.
    • This does not mean that there is no intra-group conflict, though. Conflict always exists!
    • Yet, even in times of conflict, there is some degree of mutual intelligibility provided by a shared cultural universe.
  • Culture is naturalised
    • We come to feel that our culturally-determined way of doing things is natural, inevitable, and necessary.
    • It feels strange to do things any other way.
    • This may make life far more manageable. But it “blinds” us to the constructed and arbitrary nature of our own practices and beliefs.
  • Culture is integrated
    • All aspects of a culture are related to one another
    • This interrelation is based on certain core values
    • the key and central values that glue different aspects of culture together.
    • A change in one is likely to bring about a change in others.
  • Culture is dynamic
    • It changes over time; it is not static.
    • Change can come from outside (diffusion or domination) or inside (inventions or social struggles)
      • Gender Roles!
    • Ex: Table Manners
      • Eating at a table
      • Is it a higher or advanced cultural behaviour?
      • Is it culturally superior to the other forms of eating, such as sitting on the floor?
      • Eating with forks and knives - has it always been a sign of civility? What about eating with hands?
  • Q: Culture - exists in multiplicities. Within culture, and between individuals of the same culture, there is still significant variation in their systems of meanings; for example, there is even contestation within cultures. Where do we draw the lines between one culture and another? At what point do we consider cultures different from one another?
  • Q: If some species exhibit more culture than others, what would an animal that is more cultural than humans are, look like?
  • Q: Process of emergence of culture

Culture Shock

  • A feeling of disorientation a person may feel when experiencing an unfamiliar way of life due to immigration, a visit to a new country, or simply transition to another type of life.
  • The anxiety & uncertainty of operating within a different and unknown cultural environment.
  • Getting surprised, confused, or shocked by different beliefs, practices, and lifestyles.
  • The process of Acculturation —> Learning of appropriate behavior of one's host culture
    • process of overcoming culture shock
  • Anthropologists study cultures, frequently (but not always) different from their own.

Ethnographic Research

  • Ethnography
    • Ethnos: Folk/ people &
    • Graphein: Writing
  • One of the leading people in sociocultural anthropology: Bronislaw Malinowski (1884 - 1942)
  • two meanings; refers to both the research method and the written end product
  • The Ethnographic Method, and Fieldwork
    • The immersion of investigators in the lives of the people they are trying to understand
    • The attainment of some level of understanding of the meanings those people ascribe to their existence
    • Spending extensive amounts of time with the culture under study
    • Ethnographic methods:
      • Participant observation
      • Interviews
      • Life histories, oral histories
      • Genealogies
      • Focus groups
      • Longitudinal studies (means ”long-term” or ” over time”)
      • Taking fieldnotes

  • The study of culture is not like the study of natural phenomena.
  • As a cultural being, you try to analyze culture.
  • Just like an atom trying to analyze a molecule!
  • Anthropologists try to learn a culture from the inside and acquire insider perspectives to understand local beliefs and practices in their own socio-cultural context.
  • The insider’s point of view
    • This generally requires long term residence and interaction with the people ”studied”
      • living with group members, observing and participating in daily tasks
    • Anthropologists have to learn from their interlocutors the cultural logic of seemingly strange beliefs and practices.
  • As an anthropologist, you cannot be a detached observer.
    • You have to be a participant-observer
    • Cannot simply observe people from a distance
    • Have to participate in their interlocuters' everyday lives
    • Very important to inform the people you are interacting with about your objective, and to obtain consent.

The Goal of Ethnography

Bronislav Malinovski (1884—1942) defined the goal of ethnography as:

“[to] grasp the native’s point of view, his relation to life, to realize his vision of his world.”
  • How do people understand what they are doing?
  • How do they see the world?

Perspectives

  • Etic view of a culture is the perspective of an outsider looking in.
  • Emic view of culture is often considered an “insider’s perspective.”
    • focuses on the intrinsic cultural distinctions that are meaningful to the members of a given society
    • It enables us to understand human cultural diversity in all its complexity.
    • It enables us better understand the contingency of our own cultural behaviors, dispositions and values, as well as to develop a self-critical approach to the very “cultural beings” we are.
    • It helps us to overcome the traps of
      • cross-cultural misunderstandings
      • naive realism
      • Ethnocentrism
    • Understanding something does not mean accepting it; nevertheless, it helps appreciate and respect differences, different forms of life, and ways of being.
  • The work of anthropologists is to look beyond the world of everyday experiences and appearances to discover the patterns and meanings of social life.
    • To study relationships between different and seemingly distinct elements in a whole way of life
    • To combine etic and emic perspectives

Reading Culture

  • Reading cultures as ”texts,” similar to literary texts
  • It aims at deciphering the meanings embedded in and conveyed through language, objects, gestures, and activities that members of a society share.
  • For example, this image of a chair allows us to read the following:
    • Control of the body
    • Focus of activity
    • Hierarchy
    • Uniformity
    • No privacy
    • Discipline
    • Time & space
    • Education
  • “Thick description”
    • Clifford Geertz, Interpretation of Cultures (1973)
    • The task and work of anthropologist / ethnographer as producing ”thick description”
    • Intensive, small-scale, dense descriptions of social life from observation, through which broader cultural interpretations and generalizations can be made

  • Significance of the Emic perspective
    • Enables us to understand human cultural diversity in all its complexity, temporal and spatial contingency and dynamism.
    • But also...
    • Enables us to develop a better understanding of the contingency of our own cultural behaviors, dispositions and values.
    • As well as to develop a self-critical approach to the very “cultural beings” we are.
  • Ethnocentrism
    • The tendency to judge the beliefs and behaviours of other cultures from the perspective of one’s own culture.
    • Ethnocentric Fallacy: The mistaken notion that the beliefs and behaviours of other cultures can be judged from the perspective of one’s own culture
    • Ethnocentrism can be a profound form of ignorance and intolerance.
    • It might result in great harm:
      • Ethnocentric sees no value but inferiority in other peoples and cultures
      • Ethnocentrism de-humanizes other peoples and cultures
      • Ethnocentric ideas often pave the way to unjust, unethical, or murderous practices, such as racism, slavery, and genocide.
  • Cultural relativism
    • Understand beliefs and behaviours of another culture in terms of the culture they are found
    • not to make judgements using the standards of one’s own culture.
    • To understand another culture’s practice simply means that you try to see how it fits into their lives, worldview, beliefs, values.
  • Critical cultural relativism
    • An alternative perspective on cultural relativism that poses questions about cultural beliefs and practices in terms of:
      • Who accepts them and Why
      • who might be disproportionately harming and benefiting,
      • and the cultural power dynamics that enable them (p16)

Kinship

  • Humans live in groups. As a species, we rarely live alone or in isolation.
  • Kinship is perhaps the most effective strategy humans have developed to form stable, reliable, separate, and deeply connected groups that can last over time and through generations.
  • Kinship is the system of meaning and power created to determine who is related to whom and to define their mutual expectations, rights, and responsibilities.
    • Who is “related” to whom?
    • Who you’re related to and how you’re related to them!
    • What does or does not count as a family
    • Who actually constitutes a family?
    • Who decides?
    • Is kinship biological or chosen?
  • Nuclear Family: The kinship unit of mother, father, and children.
    • Cross-cultural studies have shown that this is a Euro-American ideal!
  • Kinship groups come in a variety of shapes and sizes:
    • We trace our connections through biological ancestors.
    • We create kinship relations through marriage and remarriage.
    • We adopt. We foster.
    • We even imagine everyone in our nation to be part of one big, related, kinship community.
    • Although the term kinship may be unfamiliar to you, the subject material is not.
  • Kinship is deeply intertwined with forces beyond the everyday activities of family and home.
    • In our families, we also learn basic patterns of human behaviour: how to treat one another, how to act in groups, how to navigate differences of age, gender, ethnicity, and sexuality.
    • The enculturation in families shapes the ways we think about gender roles, the division of labor, religious practices, warfare, politics, migration, and nationalism.
    • The study of kinship is one of anthropology’s unique innovations for thinking about how culture works.
  • Cultures have a variety of ways of organizing kinship relationships. Some are familiar to you, and others are not, but all are equally valid.
  • One way that humans construct kinship groups is by tracking genealogical descent.
  • Basic kinship ties
    • Consanguinity refers to kinship relationships based on birth. (related by “blood”)
    • Affinity refers to kinship relationships based on marriage, also can include non-biological relationships. Choosing family also part of this.

Descent

  • Descent refers to relatedness based on common ancestry.
  • Descent is a kinship rule that ties people together based on reputed common ancestry.
  • Rules of descent determine
    • the descent group to which one belongs
    • the systems of inheritance of property

Descent Groups

  • Descent group is a kinship group in which primary relationships are traced through certain consanguineous (“blood”) relatives.
  • These would include your mother, father, sister, brother, grandparents, children, and grandchildren, as well as your uncles and aunts who are your parents’ siblings — but not your uncles and aunts who are married to your parents’ siblings.
  • Descent group is central to the social structure of most nonindustrial cultures outside Europe and North America.
  • Descent groups may be matrilineal or patrilineal.

Lineage:

  • A type of descent group that traces genealogical connection (matrilineal or patrilineal) through generations by linking persons to a founding ancestor.

Clan:

  • A type of descent group based on a claim to a founding ancestor but lacking genealogical documentation.
  • Types of descent
    • Patrilineal kinship is a system of descent in which persons are related to their kin through the father only. (p. 131)
      • Ex. Rural Chinese family
    • Matrilineal kinship is a system of descent in which persons are related to their kin through the mother only. (p. 131)
      • Ex. Trobriand Islanders
    • These are unilineal descent.
    • Bilateral kinship is a system in which individuals trace their descent through both parents. (p. 130)
      • Examples:
      • A majority of Canadians observe bilateral descent. Property inheritance from father and mother.
      • Ju/’hoansi are bilaterally related
      • Yako of Nigeria- Rights to farmland, forest produce, and religious offices derive from the father’s family while accumulated wealth like currency, livestock and household goods are inherited from the mother’s family.
  • Descent and residence patterns
    • Societies with patrilineal descent mostly have patrilocal residence
      • the wives live with or near the families of their husbands after marriage.
    • In some societies with matrilineal descent, walking marriage is practiced. In others, matrilocal residence or neolocal residence are practiced
      • Matrilocal residence: the husbands live with or near the families of their wives after marriage
      • Neolocal residence: married couple setting up a new household is practiced.

Descent and Relations

  • Role of the father and the mother’s brother
  • Patrilineal Kinship → Father is the male authority figure.
  • Matrilineal Kinship → Mother’s brother is the male authority figure. Relations with the father are limited.
  • Example: Among Hopi, relationship with mother’s brother is characterised by reserve, respect and fear and those with fathers are affectionate and involves less discipline.

Marriage and Affinal Ties

  • A second way humans form kinship groups is through marriage— affinal relationships.
  • A kinship relationship established through marriage and/or alliance, not through biology or common descent.
  • Marriage extends social alliances by linking together different families and kin groups
  • Connection is through affinity and alliance, not through shared biology and common descent.

Marriage

  • Marriage is a socially recognized relationship that may involve
    • physical and emotional intimacy.
    • sexual pleasure
    • reproduction and raising of children
    • mutual support and companionship
    • shared legal rights to property and inheritance
  • Arranged Marriage is a marriage orchestrated by the families of the involved parties.
  • Companionate marriage is a marriage built on love, intimacy, and personal choice rather than social obligation.
  • Marriage rules
    • Cultural rules, often inscribed in law, may determine who is a legitimate or preferred marriage partner.
    • They may even determine how many people one can marry.
    • Exogamy, Endogamy, Polygyny, Polyandry, Monogamy
    • Exogamy is marriage rule which requires one to marry outside one’s own group.
      • Example: Trobriand Islanders or Bhils depicted in the article. Family and Kinship in Village India are village exogamous and clan exogamous. They are supposed to marry spouses from other villages.
    • Endogamy is a marriage rule which requires marriage inside one’s own group.
      • Example: Bhils depicted in the article Family and Kinship in Village India are supposed to marry within their own ethnic group, meaning other Bhils.
  • Monogamy is a marriage rule that permits an individual to marry only one other person at a time.
  • Polygamy is a form of marriage in which one person is permitted to have more than one spouse
  • Polygyny is a form of marriage in which a man is permitted to have more than one wife. (p. 145)
  • Polyandry is a form of marriage in which a woman is permitted to have more than one husband. (p. 146)
  • Bridewealth: The valuables that a groom or his family are expected or obligated to present to the bride’s family. (p. 140)
  • Dowry: The goods and valuables that the bride’s family supplies to the groom’s family or the couple. (p.143)

Incest Taboo

  • All cultures have some form of Incest Taboo
  • Cultural rule that forbids sexual relations with certain close relatives.
  • A rule that prohibits sexual relations among kin of certain categories, such as brothers or sisters, parents and children, or, in some cases, cousins. (p. 138)
  • Such taboos relate to nuclear family members. Incest taboos also affect marriage patterns.
  • Incest taboos universally prohibit marriage between siblings and between parents and children, between grandparents and grandchildren.

Lecture 6 — February 17 2022 | Religion, Ritual, Symbol

  • Is there something present in all cultures that we can call “religion”?
  • Humans experience religion physically and express it through their actions.
  • We may study a religion’s history, theology, scriptures, and major figures, but we do so to understand their meaning and significance in the life of a community of people.

What is religion?

Some linear combination of the following 7 elements:

  1. Belief in powers or deities whose abilities transcend those of the natural world
  1. Myths and stories that reflect on the meaning and purpose of life, its origins, and humans’ place in the universe.
  1. Ritual activities that reinforce, recall, instill, and explore collective beliefs
  1. Powerful symbols that represent key aspects of the religion for its followers.
  1. Specialists who assist the average believer to bridge everyday life experiences and the religion’s ideals and supernatural aspects.
  1. Organizations and institutions that preserve, explore, teach, and implement the religion’s key beliefs.
  1. A community of believers
  • Anthropologists have largely been uninterested in questions of any religion’s ultimate truth or falsity.
  • Instead, we understand that religious worlds are real, meaningful, and powerful to those who live in them.
  • Our task is to carefully make those worlds come alive for others by capturing their:
    • vivid inner life,
    • sense of moral order,
    • dynamic public expressions,
    • interactions with other systems of meaning and power

Some Theories on Religion

  • EB. Tyler and James Frazer
    • Religion is the belief in spiritual beings
    • It exists to help people explain events which would otherwise be incomprehensible.
  • Sigmund Freud
    • Religion is a form of mass neurosis, a byproduct of psychological distress.
    • It exists only as a response to deep emotional conflicts and weaknesses.
  • Emile Durkheim:
    • Religion is a unified system of beliefs and practices relative to sacred things, that is to say, things set apart and forbidden.
    • Religious beliefs are thus symbolic expressions of social realities — without those social realities serving as a foundation, religious beliefs would have no meaning.
    • Totemism: The use of symbol, generally an animal or a plant, as a physical representation for a group, generally a clan. (p. 95)
    • The totem is worshipped and considered sacred and holy by the members of the group.
    • It serves as a concrete representation of the group.
  • Working definitions:
    • Religion: A set of beliefs and rituals based on a unique vision of how the world ought to be, often focused on a supernatural power and lived out in community.
    • Spradley and McCurdy define religion as “the cultural knowledge of the supernatural that people use to cope with the ultimate problems of human existence.”
      • allows people to grapple with why they are and who they are.
  • Clifford Geertz
    • Religion is a system of symbols.
    • They persuade us that there is a direct connection between our WORLDVIEW (how the world is) and our ETHOS (how we live or ought to live).
    • Worldview: An encompassing picture of reality based on shared cultural assumptions about how the world works. (p. 94)
    • Religious symbols tell us that we ought to live a certain way because the world is a certain way.
    • In religious symbols, worldview and ethos seem to fit together perfectly, so each reinforces the other.
    • Religion is (Clifford Geetz, Interpretation of Cultures, 1973):
      1. a system of symbols which acts to
      1. establish powerful, pervasive, and long-lasting moods and motivations in human beings by
      1. formulating conceptions of a general order of existence and
      1. clothing these conceptions with such an aura of factuality that
      1. the moods and motivations seem uniquely realistic.
    • Geertz: symbols are vehicles of meaning.
      • Culture is not locked in people’s heads; it is embodied in symbolic communication
      • People communicate their worldview, values, ethos and other important things through symbols
      • How symbols carry meaning and shape the ways people see, feel, think about the world?
    • Symbolic Action
      • People communicate, perpetuate, and develop their knowledge about and attitudes toward life through symbolic actions
      • Symbolic actions are activities not only expressing a particular view of the world but also they effectively shape action
      • Examples of symbolic action are rituals, games, performances
  • Victor Turner
    • Symbols as Operators in the Social Process
    • How do symbols perform certain practical functions for social groups in social processes?
    • Symbolic Action: Turnerian View
      • Symbols are processes and behaviors, not just objects or words
      • Symbols can do more than just carry meaning; they can also make things happen
      • Symbols can have practical social effects; perform practical operations in the social process
    • “operators in the social process: i.e., things that, when put together in certain arrangements and contexts (especially in rituals) produce social transformations.” (Turner 1967:87)
    • How is it that solidarity in groups is constructed and maintained over and above conflicts that constitute normal state of affairs?
    • Turner explored symbols in Ndembu curing and initiation rituals to understand how these rituals
      • move actors from one status to another
      • resolve social contradictions
      • wed people to the categories and norms of their society.
    • For Turner, symbols are the smallest meaningful unit of ritual which reveal crucial social values of the community

Social Hierarchy and Stratification

  • Some notion of social hierarchy is found in nearly all human societies. However, stratification is not a universal phenomenon.
  • Egalitarian society
    • “A group based on the sharing of resources to ensure success with a relative absence of hierarchy and violence.” (Guest, p. 264)

      • Ex: Hunter-gatherer societies do not have social classes, often lack permanent leaders, and actively avoid locating their members into hierarchical power positions.
      • Remember Richard Lee’s Christmas Dinner in Kalahari
  • Rank society
    • A group in which wealth is not stratified but prestige and status are.” (Guest, p. ZéS)
    • Unequal access to prestige, but not economic resources.
    • Village Head, Big Man, and inriz‘aal leader have authority and prestige but no system of privilege to access economic resources.
      • Ex: Horticulturalists and early chiefdoms.
      • Remember Ongka's Big Moka
  • Slavery
    • A system of inequality in which some people are owned as property and are forced to work for others.
    • Today, legally banned in nearly all countries.
  • Caste System in India
    • Individuals are assigned at birth to their parents’ ranked social and occupational groups of their parents.
    • A person’s place in the social order is relatively fixed; there is little mobility from one caste to another
    • Castes are separated from each other by strict rules that forbid intermarriage and other forms of interaction
  • Apartheid in South Africa (1948-1990s)
    • A system of stratification based on the rigid separation of racial and ethnic groups

    • Blacks lacked the right to vote and were segregated into ghettos, only allowed out to work for whites.
  • Class System in Capitalist Societies
    • “A system of power based on wealth, income, and status that creates an unequal distribution of a society’s resources.” (Guest, p. Zéfi)
    • A social class is a large group of people who occupy a similar economic position in the wider society.
    • Most social scientists identify social classes in terms of wealth and income, noting how social class makes a difference in terms of consumption, education, health, social position, and access to political power.
    • Defining Class
      • Karl Marx defines class in terms of the ownership of means of production (land, factories, tools, and raw materials needed for production).
      • Class societies are divided between
        • Ruling classes (those who own the means of production and control the labor power of others)
        • Lower classes (those who work for the ruling classes).

Integrative Theory of Social Stratification

  • The integrative approach regards social hierarchy to be inevitable and necessary for social integration and cohesion.
  • As societies grow, they develop more labor-efficient means to produce enough food and other necessities; specialization takes hold.
  • A division of labor follows for effective and efficient organization and coordination of production, distribution, and infrastructural requirements.
  • The emergence of complex leadership systems is to regulate the conduct of social and political life as well as defense.
  • Metaphor: Society as ‘a living organism’ functioning with interrelated parts all doing their jobs and regulated by controlling device.
  • Herbert Spencer, mm c. philosopher, biologist, anthropologist, suggested that complex societies, like complex living organisms, exhibit greater differentiation as they evolve.
    • Greater differentiation leads to a greater degree of interrelation among parts
    • which in turn requires greater control by government, management, and the military.
    • Without control, society, like a living organism, would cease to exist.

Exploitative Theory of Social Stratification

  • Other scholars agree that complex societies require integration, but they disagree that integration requires social hierarchy.
  • Proponents of Exploitative Theory claim that stratification arises when one group seeks to exploit the resources or labor of others
  • The exploitation might take the form of military conquest, as it did during colonialism.
  • Or it might take other forms of manipulation and control.
  • Regards social hierarchy as neither unavoidable nor necessary; it has nothing to do with human nature.
  • Social inequality exists because one group of individuals seek to exert their power onto others in order to further their economic and political interests.
  • The most influential proponents of exploitative theories of social stratification are Karl Marx, Friedrich Engels, and Max Weber.
  • Marx and Engels regard social classes as the primary basis of social stratification. (more about this later)

Social Classes Today

  • Industrialized societies are divided between:
    • The upper class (business or finance elites like factory and bank owners, politicians, managers)
    • The middle class
      • Upper-middle (high-income professionals, small business owners)
      • Lower-middle (white-collar workers)
    • The lower class (blue-collar or service workers, urban poor)
    • The underclass (marginalized urban or rural poor who have no employment opportunity)
  • Theoretically speaking, the class system is more open than other systems of social stratification.
    • Class systems are more fluid and less clear-cut. Classes are neither legally nor religiously sanctioned nor hereditary in biological terms.
    • Class positions are in some part achieved. Upward/downward social mobility is possible to an extent.
    • Social mobility is “the movement of one’s class position, upward or downward, in stratified societies.” (Guest, p. 268)
    • In contrast with slavery and caste systems, classes are economically based and impersonal. (Yet, other axes of inequality such as race and gender continue to shape one’s class position.)
  • The Theory of Intersectionality
    • Individuals and their lives are affected, defined, or determined by the statuses they hold in relation to the contexts they are in.
    • Intersectionality is “an analytic framework for assessing how factors such as race, gender, and class interact to shape individual life chances and societal patterns of stratification.” (Guest, p. 271)
    • Example: A working-class woman faces economic and gender discrimination
    • A working-class woman of color faces economic, gender, and racial discrimination.
    • Various axes of inequality (such as class, race, gender, ethnicity, sexual orientation, or disability) constantly interact with each other.
    • These different forms of social hierarchies and inequality create "intersections” of multiple forms of discrimination.
    • Racial, ethnic, cultural minority groups, and within them, women, children, the disabled, etc., tend to disproportionately occupy the lower ranks of the society.
    • The intersection of Class, Race, and Gender
      • In today’s North American societies race/ethnicity and gender provide the most significant (though not exclusive) mediums for the perpetuation and reproduction of social inequality and stratification.
      • That is regardless of individual intentions, inequality on the basis of race/ethnicity and gender continues to limit different groups’ access to social mobility (wealth, jobs, education, health care, and so on).

Lecture 12 — April 7 2022 | Globalisation and Neoliberalism

  • Globalization: The worldwide intensification of interactions and increased movement of money, people, goods, and ideas within and across national borders. (Guest 2018:18).
  • “the intensification of worldwide social relations that link distant localities in such a way that local happenings are shaped by events occurring many miles away, and Vice versa.” (p. 214)
  • Arjun Appadurai (2006) discusses these effects in terms of global cultural flows, where there exists an increasing exchange and flow of information across five different interconnected dimensions, or what Appadurai calls “scapes”
    • mediascapes, ethnoscapes, financescapes, technoscapes, and ideoscapes.
  • Globalization is not an entirely new phenomenon.
  • Intensification of global interaction occurred in earlier eras as breakthroughs in communication and transportation brought the world’s people into closer contact.
  • The present period of globalization, however, has reached a level of intensity previously unknown.
  • As Roxana Ng (2002, 74) points out, “what is new in this era of globalization is the ability of capital to move sites of production across national borders with relative ease.”
  • Globalization today is characterized by several key dynamics, all of which are happening at an increasingly rapid pace:
    • Time-space compression
      • The rapid innovation of communication and transportation technologies has transformed the way we think about space (distances) and time.
      • Jet travel, superhighways, high-speed railways, telephones, computers, the Internet, digital cameras, and cell phones have condensed time and space,
      • Changes in our sense of how long it takes to do something and how far away someplace or someone is.
    • Flexible accumulation
      • It refers to the increasingly flexible strategies that corporations use to accumulate profits in an era of globalization.
      • In particular, these strategies include
        • offshoring: relocating factories anywhere in the world that provides optimal production, infrastructure, labor, marketing, and political conditions
        • outsourcing: hiring low-wage laborers in periphery countries to perform jobs previously done in core countries.
      • By so doing, corporations could bypass high production costs, organized labor, and environmental laws in developed countries.
    • Increasing Migration
      • the accelerated movement of people both within and between countries.
      • International migration of more than 232 million people (UN ZOB)
      • An estimated 700 million more are internal migrants within their own countries, usually moving from rural to urban areas in search of work.
    • Uneven development
      • Although many people associate globalization with rapid economic development and progress, globalization has not brought equal benefits to the world’s people.
      • The new technologies associated with globalization may indeed allow more and more people to interact and communicate and led to economic expansion, but billions of other people are being left out of these advances.
      • In other words, along with the economic expansion and growth associated with globalization, there are equally significant global economic inequalities.

Neoliberalism

  • “An economic philosophy that argues for minimal government involvement in the economy and greatly accelerated economic growth. Well—being, neoliberals argue, is best served by liberating individual entrepreneurs to operate in a framework of strong property rights, free markets, and free trade.” (p. 221)
  • An ideological and philosophical movement that emerged at a particular historical moment
  • It can be traced to the networks of specific intellectuals and institutions in post-World War I Europe and the United States.
  • This economic and political worldview sees the free market as the main mechanism for ensuring economic growth, with a severely restricted role for government.
  • The role of the state is limited to the creation and preservation of an institutional framework appropriate to such practices
  • Neoliberal policies focus on promoting
    • free trade on a global scale, eliminating trade barriers, and reducing taxes, tariffs, and most government intervention in the economy.
      • It may also mean reducing environmental or social laws when they restrict the flow of goods and capital. (p. 222)
      • The state should also open markets in areas such as education, water, land, health care, and social security.
      • The state intervention should be kept to a minimum
    • privatization of public assets and an overall reduction, if not privatization, of government spending on health, education, and welfare.
  • Neoliberal Policies
    • Since the 1980s, international financial institutions, the International Monetary Fund (IMF), the World Bank, and the World Trade Organization (WT O), have used structural adjustment loans (debt) to address poverty and development in developing countries.
      • fiscal austerity measures,
      • a reduced role for the state in the economy,
      • a restructuring of national trade and tariff policies
    • After WW2, restructuring of state forms and international relations was designed to prevent a return to the catastrophic conditions that had threatened the capitalist order and led to world wars.
      • Internationally, new world order was constructed through the Bretton Woods agreements (1944), and various institutions like the United Nations, the World Bank and the IMF.
      • With Bretton Woods, free trade in goods was encouraged under a system of fixed exchange rates anchored by the US dollar’s convertibility into gold at a fixed price (known as the gold standard).
      • This system existed under the umbrella protection of US economic and military power.
  • Embedded Liberalism
    • Post-WWII System (1945-1980) known as embedded liberalism was characterized by a blend of state, market, and democratic institutions to guarantee peace, inclusion, well—being and stability
    • A ‘class compromise’ between capital and labor was considered the key guarantor of domestic peace and tranquillity
    • The state thus focused on full employment, economic growth, and the welfare of its citizens, and it was generally accepted that state power should be freely deployed to achieve these ends
    • In the advanced capitalist countries, redistributive politics, controls over the free mobility of capital, expanded public expenditures and welfare state-building, and active state interventions in the economy went hand in hand with relatively high rates of growth during the 1950s and 1960s.
    • (Before the Neoliberal turn) By the end of the 1960s, embedded liberalism began to break down.
    • The crisis of «embedded liberalism» was the low-profit crisis - wages were "too high," and the rate of profit was low, causing a recession.
    • Also widespread discontent: social movements across Europe and the world pointing toward a socialist alternative to the embedded liberalism
    • There was a clear political threat to economic elites and ruling classes everywhere, both in the advanced capitalist countries and in many developing countries
  • The solution to the above
    • The proposed/ chosen solution was globalization and neoliberalism
    • Globalization: the US ended the Bretton Woods System in 1971, allowing the dollar to fluctuate
    • Competition among the states to attract capital
    • In the Global North — the end of the social contract between labour and capital

      • the deregulation of the economy, the process of removing or reducing state regulations
      • the liberalization of trade and industry; removing barriers to trade between different countries, and encouraging free trade
